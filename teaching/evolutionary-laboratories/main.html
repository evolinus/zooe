The Branching Room

One ancestor, copied and mutated generation after generation, occasionally splits into two independent lineages. Neither split is steered toward any goal — each new branch just keeps drifting on its own.

A BRANCHING COPY MACHINE — Ancestor A splits into B and C at Generation 0 (fixed). The three remaining split generations — B→D,E · E→F,G · F→H,I — are set by the sliders above and must stay in order, each at least 5 generations apart. The final lineages (C, D, G, H, I) each keep mutating independently until generation N. Every split point remains visible afterward as a frozen snapshot of the ancestor.

THE WRIGHT-FISHER MODEL — The first 3 generations are animated in real-time, sampling individuals one by one with replacement using a roulette wheel proportional to the current allele frequency. Subsequent generations are still calculated instantly, but stepped through at about 5 generations per second so you can follow the drift. Observe how smaller population sizes (N) cause the frequency to drift more erratically, leading to faster fixation or loss. Switching to Diploid treats N as the number of diploid individuals (2N gene copies total) rather than N haploid gene copies — the same drift process, just with twice as many genes buffering it, so frequencies wander more slowly and fixation takes longer on average.

The Selection Room

The same finite-population sampling as the Drift Room, but allele A now carries a fitness advantage: individuals carrying it are more likely to be copied into the next generation. Watch how selection strength (s) and population size (N) compete with random drift.

SELECTION + DRIFT — Allele A has relative fitness (1+s) against B's fitness of 1. Each generation, the raw frequency f is reweighted to f′ = f(1+s) / (f(1+s) + (1−f)) before the usual binomial sampling — so f′, not f, is what the wheel spins on and what the binomial panel draws from. The dashed line on the frequency chart is the deterministic path selection alone would take with no sampling noise; the actual jagged line shows how much drift pulls it off course. Larger N·s means selection dominates and A reliably fixes; smaller N·s means drift can still overpower a real fitness advantage, occasionally fixing B instead. Switching to Diploid changes the biology, not just the bookkeeping: fitness now belongs to genotypes (AA, AB, BB) rather than lone alleles, weighted by dominance h — h=0.5 is additive, h=1 makes A fully dominant, h=0 makes it fully recessive. A recessive advantageous allele hides inside heterozygotes while rare, so selection barely "sees" it — it can still be lost to drift almost as easily as a neutral allele, even with a real fitness edge.

The Adaptation Room

Three founder fish — Gigi, Mario, and Nani — each split into a neutral lineage and a habitat-assigned lineage. Five traits (shape, colour, eye size, fin size, tail size) each mutate and fix independently under selection + drift, exactly as in the Selection Room, one trait "locus" at a time. Watch whether habitat-driven selection reaches a characteristic look faster, and more reliably, than drift alone.

HABITAT-DRIVEN ADAPTATION — Each of the 5 traits is its own independent locus: while ancestral, it has a chance each generation (its mutation rate μ) of producing a new variant, which then runs a real diploid Wright-Fisher trajectory (selection + drift, full dominance) until it fixes or is lost. A mutation is only ever selected for or against relative to what its habitat wants — Stream favors slender, bluish, long-tailed, short-finned fish; Pond favors round, reddish fish with long, less-forked tails and long fins; River favors greenish fish with dark fins and big eyes, with no shape preference. Neutral lineages run the identical genetic machinery with every selection coefficient at zero — any difference you see between a founder's Neutral and Habitat lineage is what selection alone contributes on top of drift.
